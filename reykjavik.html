Reykjavik, Iceland — Day 1

A city built from basalt & light

A downhill route through the capital — from the church that mimics Iceland's lava columns, past a Viking ship of steel, to a hall of glass built to catch the sky.

Roughly 2 mi / 3 km · mostly downhill
The Route

Six stops, downhill to the water

1

Hallgrímskirkja Church & Rainbow Street

Start at Hallgrímskirkja, the towering concrete church that defines the city's skyline, its design echoing Iceland's natural basalt lava columns. Step inside to see the 25-ton pipe organ. Then walk straight down Skólavörðustígur — Rainbow Street — painted in vibrant colors leading toward the church.

Optional: the tower observation deck for the best view of Reykjavik's rooftops (small fee).
2

Sun Voyager & Waterfront Stroll

From the bottom of Rainbow Street, cut north toward the ocean (about 10 minutes) to reach the Sun Voyager (Sólfarið) — a striking stainless-steel sculpture resembling a Viking longship, looking out across Faxaflói Bay toward Mount Esja. Continue west along the paved seaside path toward the harbor, about 10 brisk, scenic minutes.

3

Harpa Concert Hall

Arrive at Harpa, a masterpiece of modern architecture wrapped in over 10,000 geometric glass panels designed by artist Ólafur Elíasson to mimic the basalt landscape and capture the shifting Icelandic light. The foyer is free to enter — honeycomb windows cast kaleidoscopic patterns on the floor. Head upstairs for great views.

4

Old Harbor

Walk out of Harpa and amble into the historic Old Harbor district.

5

Parliament Square & Tjörnin Lake

Head slightly inland to Austurvöllur Square, the public heart of the city, bordered by the stone Parliament Building (Alþingi) and Reykjavik Cathedral. A minute south, find Tjörnin (The Pond) — a peaceful city lake ringed by old houses and busy with swans and ducks.

6

Reykjavik City Hall

Finish inside Reykjavik City Hall, right on the edge of the water. The lobby holds a massive, highly detailed 3D topographical map of Iceland.

Why the glass looks like that

Harpa's facade is more than decoration — its 10,000-plus geometric panels are a deliberate echo of Iceland's basalt columns, refracting daylight (and the aurora, in season) into shifting color across the foyer floor.
