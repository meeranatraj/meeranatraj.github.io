Day 1

Around Reykjavik

A downhill route through the capital, mostly on foot.

Distance ~2 mi / 3 kmTerrain Mostly downhill
1

Hallgrímskirkja Church & Rainbow Street

The towering concrete church that defines the skyline, its design echoing Iceland's basalt lava columns. Inside is a 25-ton pipe organ. Walk out front and down Skólavörðustígur — Rainbow Street — painted in vibrant colors leading toward the church.

Tower observation deck: small fee
2

Sun Voyager & Waterfront Stroll

From the bottom of Rainbow Street, cut north toward the ocean (~10 min) to the Sun Voyager (Sólfarið), a stainless-steel sculpture resembling a Viking longship, facing Faxaflói Bay toward Mount Esja. Continue west along the seaside path toward the harbor, another ~10 minutes.

3

Harpa Concert Hall

Wrapped in over 10,000 geometric glass panels designed by Ólafur Elíasson to mimic the basalt landscape and catch the shifting light. The foyer is free to enter — honeycomb windows cast kaleidoscopic patterns on the floor. Worth heading upstairs for the view.

4

Old Harbor

Walk out of Harpa and amble into the historic Old Harbor district.

5

Parliament Square & Tjörnin Lake

Austurvöllur Square, the public heart of the city, bordered by the stone Parliament Building (Alþingi) and Reykjavik Cathedral. A minute south is Tjörnin (The Pond), ringed by old houses and busy with swans and ducks.

6

Reykjavik City Hall

Right on the water's edge — the lobby holds a large, highly detailed 3D topographical map of Iceland.

Day 2

The Golden Circle

A 270 km loop east of Reykjavik — around 3 hr 59 min of driving in total, before stops.

Distance 270 kmDrive time ~4 hr
1

Þingvellir National Park

45 minutes from Reykjavik. Birthplace of Iceland's ancient parliament, Alþingi, founded in 930. The Visitor Center sits near the P1 parking lot; from there, the Almannagja Gorge walk is one of the clearest places to see the North American and Eurasian tectonic plates meet.

Around the site: the Silfra fissure (east of the center), a bridge over the Peningagjá fissure (north), Þingvellir Church (straight ahead), Snorri's Booth and the supposed Law Rock site, Drekkingarhylur pond, and — further north — Öxarárfoss waterfall. Þingvallavatn, Iceland's largest natural lake, feeds the Silfra fissure.

Plan ~1.5 hr
Parking: P1 (Hakid) is the main entry point at the top of Almannagja Gorge. P2 sits below Öxarárfoss, P3 is 900m north of the falls, and P5 is closest to the church and Silfra Fissure.
2

Efstidalur Dairy Farm

Between Þingvellir and Geysir — a farm-to-table ice cream stop, open from 10am.

3

Haukadalur Geothermal Area

40 minute drive. Home to the dormant Great Geysir and the active Strokkur, which erupts every 4–10 minutes.

Plan ~1 hr
4

Gullfoss Falls

10 minutes from Geysir. The upper parking lot sits near the Visitor Center (restrooms, shop, café); from there a short hike leads down to a closer viewpoint. The full round-trip trail is about 2 km (1.2 mi), 15–30 minutes. The Geysir Center across the road has restaurants and Hotel Geysir.

Plan ~1 hr
5

Faxi Waterfall

Featured in Game of Thrones. From Gullfoss, continue on Route 36, then east 17.3 km, left onto Route 48, and another 5.6 km to the marked exit. A short walk from the parking lot reaches the viewpoint.

6

Friðheimar Tomato Farm & Restaurant

A family-run tomato farm and restaurant right off the Golden Circle route (watch for the sign — easy to miss). About 30 minutes from Geysir via Route 35 / Biskupstungnabraut. Reasonably priced — soup with home-baked bread and coffee or tea runs around 2,000 ISK.

7

Secret Lagoon

30 minutes from Gullfoss, near Flúðir. Iceland's oldest natural pool, dating to 1891, set in a geothermal area with a small erupting geyser nearby.

Reservation suggested — evening slot, e.g. 6pm
8

Kerið Crater Lake

30 minutes from Secret Lagoon, near Selfoss — a fitting last stop on the loop. Also called the "Eye of the World," a red volcanic rock caldera holding a striking turquoise-blue lake.

Plan 30–40 min~700 ISK entranceFree parking

Day 3

The South Coast

East from Reykjavik on fully paved, easily navigable roads — waterfalls, glaciers, and a lagoon of drifting icebergs.

Furthest point Jökulsárlón, ~380 km
1

Seljalandsfoss Waterfall

120 km (75 mi) east of Reykjavik. A trail circles directly behind the falling water. A short walk away, Gljúfurárfoss is tucked into a crack in the cliff.

2

Skógafoss Waterfall

30 km (18 mi) past Seljalandsfoss. A towering 60-meter drop, with stairs climbing to the top.

3

Sólheimajökull Glacier

A 10 km drive off Route 1. Streaks of white, blue, and black ice form dramatic ridges and patterns; an easy 10-minute walk from the parking lot. Hvolsvöllur, along the route, is the most convenient stop for fuel, food, or restrooms.

4

Dyrhólaey Cliffs

Two parking areas — the lower one gives access to Kirkjufjara beach and the puffin viewing boardwalks. Puffins are most active in the evening.

Visitor hours are restricted during puffin season (roughly April–August); Dyrhólaey closes at 7pm.
5

Reynisfjara Black Sand Beach

Near Vík, 187 km (116 mi) from Reykjavik. Known for its basalt sea cave and the Reynisdrangar rock stacks rising from the water.

Sneaker waves: the surf here can turn fierce without warning, even on calm days — mini tsunamis that strike the shore suddenly. Stay well back from the water at all times.
6

Fjaðrárgljúfur Canyon

Iceland's most famous canyon, known for its serpentine shape — about 50 minutes east of Vík. Restrooms at the main parking lot; a short walk from the upper lot reaches the main viewpoint, and a flat path continues to a full canyon panorama.

7

Vatnajökull National Park

Home to the Skaftafell area, 325 km (202 mi) from the capital — glacier views and hiking trails.

8

Diamond Beach & Jökulsárlón Glacier Lagoon

Massive icebergs drift in the Jökulsárlón lagoon before washing ashore on the black sands of Diamond Beach, a 3-minute drive away — one of the best stops on the whole South Coast.

Before you go

Getting around

Practical notes worth knowing before day one.

Route

Golden Circle reference map

A route attraction map is worth having on hand for Day 2 — see adventures.is's Golden Circle guide for the full loop layout.

Safety

Sneaker waves at Reynisfjara

The single most important safety note on this trip — never turn your back on the ocean at Reynisfjara, and keep well clear of the waterline.

Timing

Puffin season

Roughly April to August at Dyrhólaey, with restricted visitor hours (closes 7pm) and best viewing in the evening.

Fuel & food

Hvolsvöllur, Day 3

The most convenient stop for fuel, food, or restrooms along the South Coast route.

Reservations

Secret Lagoon, Day 2

Book an evening slot in advance — 6pm is a good target so it caps off the day.

Driving

All paved, all easy

Both the Golden Circle and South Coast routes described here are fully paved and straightforward to self-drive.
